What’s the Difference?

  • Better design?
  • Professional speakers?
Why do we hate most presentations, but spend so much time watching TED talks? Is it that the slides are better designed? Or maybe it’s because the speakers seem so professional?

Nope.

Many TED speakers are pretty inexperienced, actually. Many of the presentations aren’t all that great to look at, either.

The keys to a great presentation

  • Don’t get lost in the details.
  • Focus on the benefits to the audience.
  • Don’t rely too much on your slides.
  • Learn to pretend as if you know what you’re doing.
  • ...and then there’s The Ultimate Presenters’ Tool™.

What’s The Ultimate Presenters’ Tool™, you ask?

Here’s your incentive to stay awake.

Tip #1

Don’t Get Lost in the Details

This Is My Sister

Her stories are famously terrible.
When we were kids, my mom would start a story timer and say, "Okay. You’ve got five minutes to wrap this up." Every story was a maze of tangents, useless details, and rambling nonsense. There may have been a point in there, but none of us were ever able to tease it out.

Only Include Essential Details

Choose only the right details, and the story becomes more powerful. Include too many and it becomes hard-to-follow and boring.

“But all of these details are absolutely essential to my presentation!”

You may feel like leaving out details will rob your audience of the impact, but you'd be sorely mistaken. Don't believe me? Allow me to present one of the most impactful (and complete) stories I've ever seen:

“For sale: baby shoes, never worn.”
– Ernest Hemingway (probably apocryphal)

This is a single detail, but we easily understand the entire tragic story. The origin of this story is unclear, but it's apocryphally attributed to Hemingway, who used it to win a bet that he couldn't write a full story in six words. If Hemingway can write a tragedy in six words, we can write a fucking PowerPoint slide in a sentence or two.

Tip #2

Focus on the
Benefits

People are selfish

Use That

Try to frame everything from the perspective of the person listening. How does this information help them? How can it make their lives easier? What problem has been solved for them?

Focus on Benefits in

3 Easy Steps

Step 1:

Find the Thing That Hurts

This could be something obvious that everyone complains about all the time already; or it could be something subtle that no one has thought of yet but can’t live without once they know it exists, like wheels on suitcases.

Step 2:

Poke It

Remind them how painful it is. Try to paint a mental picture of how frustrating this problem can be, or ask them to reflect on the last time they had to use the problematic thing. If you’re doing this right, someone in the audience will groan audibly.

Step 3:

Offer a Better Solution

With the pain fresh in the audience’s mind, the power of the solution is much more apparent. If I show you a blanket with sleeves, you might laugh at the idea — but if you're picturing cold arms while trying to get to your popcorn, you’re all like...

Tip #3

Don’t Rely Too Much on Your Slides

A good slide deck is a visual aid. Your presentation should still be engaging, even if the projector or screenshare gives out.

Slides are not a written report.

Don’t try to fit everything you want to say on a slide. You’re speaking to the audience; not standing there while they read.

Slides are not notes for the speaker.

If you want to make sure to remember something, use the speaker notes.

Slides are not a script.

Reading slides verbatim is distracting, boring, and a waste of time. If the audience can get the same experience simply by reading the slides, you may as well skip the talk altogether and send them a PDF instead.

Slides are not a collection of your favorite GIFs.

I love GIFs, and I use a lot of them. But if your whole deck is funny GIFs — unless that’s the schtick — you’re more likely to distract from your talk than add to it.

Tip #4

Pretend You Know What You’re Doing

Looking like a pro is easier than you might think. All it really takes is a tiny bit of practice. Like, seriously: very little practice.

Practice Don’t memorize

Actors get paid to recite memorized lines because it’s hard not to sound like a cardboard cutout when you’re reciting something from memory. So don’t worry about writing a script — just know the content.

Run Through ItOut Loud

By doing a pass out loud, you’ll notice any rough transitions, awkward pauses, useless slides, or other things that only become apparent when you’re actually giving the presentation.

RefreshBefore the Talk

“Defrosting” a talk can be harder than writing the thing in the first place.
– Lorna Mitchell

If you haven’t looked at your deck in a while, you may get tripped up by transitions or little jokes. Or you may forget an entire section, or jump ahead and end up repeating yourself — I’ve done this a lot.

Now It’s Time for The Ultimate Presenters’ Tool™

Tip #5

Tell Stories

People Respond to Emotions

Raw information is useful and extremely necessary, but it’s hard to connect with stats and numbers. It’s our responsibility as presenters to find the story in the facts, and help people feel why what we’re saying is important.

“But what if my topic is boring?”

Very few topics are inherently interesting or boring: a good storyteller can make anything sound interesting; a bad storyteller can make anything sound boring. Don’t beleive me? Let’s look at a plot synopsis or two:

A report on nominative determinism and its negative impact on socioeconomic status and mobility.

This story in Freakonomics about how names affect our ability to get a job is as memorable and engaging as it is depressing, but if we get too buried in the jargon and lose the human element, it sounds boring as hell.

The story of four strangers who take a road trip to ask a politician for things they already have.

This is one of the most beloved movies out there, but if we just stick to the facts, there’s not much to engage the audience.

A detailed account of a grumpy alcoholic's poor life choices.

Bukowski’s writing is arguably some of the best storytelling of the 20th century — but a factual overview of his body of work sounds terrible.

Good stories follow an arc

The hero gives us someone to pull for, and allows us to tie emotions to the events taking place. Without heroes, most stories start to sound like the boring history lessons from school. In business presentations, this could be a single customer, rather than a generic “our customers...”
A challenge gives us a threat or enemy to band together against. It brings us closer to the hero and helps us buy into the story. In business, this could be a customer trying to make enough money from her business to buy gifts for her family — and her business relies on our product.
Without following the hero’s preparation, the ending feels pretty anticlimactic: “Harry Potter found himself targeted by an evil wizard, but then he won the big wizard battle.” The middle part of the story helps us identify with the hero and builds suspense. For business, this could be a high-level overview of various challenges, partners, and strategies. It can also include any failed attempts along the way — this also helps build suspense.
This is the climax of the story. After all the preparation, the hero is ready to take on the challenge. This is the moment of truth, where we find out if the preparation paid off. In business, this might be where the customer puts our product to the test during a holiday promotion — and it pulls through for her. She has her best month ever and she’s able to buy gifts for her family.
After the conflict, we need to see how things shake out in the end. What does normal life look like now? This is a good place to reflect on what was learned during the project, or how we plan to provide the same results to all of our customers.

Air Your DirtyLaundry

The idea of sharing failures and shortcomings with total strangers is fucking terrifying, but try not to only put your best foot forward. Without flaws, we find it hard to relate to each other. This is why Superman is such a tough character: he doesn’t have any flaws, and that makes him seem stiff and two-dimensional. Be willing to put the bad out with the good, and people will relate to your stories far better.

Let’s Recap

How to make your next talk great:

  • Don’t get lost in the details.
  • Focus on the benefit to the audience.
  • Don’t rely too much on slides.
  • Pretend you know what you’re doing.
  • Tell stories.
