How I Cut My Hours in Half

And somehow managed
to get more done.

@jlengstorf | jason@lengstorf.com

This beard is glorious

And I nearly robbed the world of its majesty

This was not a choice

I was a workaholic

In 2012 I averaged 70 hours per week on the computer

And it wasn’t unusual to pass 90.

I woke up checking email. I worked through all of my meals. I ordered in the vast majority of my lunches and dinners. I rarely hung out with friends, and when I did, I’d often end up ducking out to take a phone call or deal with some issue.

Then it all fell apart:

Black Friday

I landed a huge contract

  • Fortune 500 company
  • High profile project
  • Charging agency rates
  • ...and rush fees

We planned it all out...

...then reality happened

But I’m not aquitter

I worked over 100 hours that week

The Business Result:

  • The client loved it
  • The project made the client a bunch of money
  • I got paid a bunch of money
  • The project won an Addy Award

The Personal Cost:

  • I missed Thanksgiving with my family
  • I slept less than 4 hours a night
  • I ate fast food delivery for every. single. meal.
  • MY FUCKING BEARD FELL OUT
I believe I had something called alopecia barbae, which causes unexplained loss of whiskers in beards. There’s no proven cause, but some research suggests stress can create symptoms. I’m an n=1 study, but I am now extremely convinced that this is true.

I was already burning out.

Losing my beard was the final straw

My decision:

Poor & healthy
is better than successful & sick

I cut back on everything:

  • I only accepted calls during business hours
  • I turned off all notifications on my phone and computer
  • I turned on Do Not Disturb mode from 6pm to 8am
  • I only checked email twice a day
  • I set up activities with my friends...
  • ...and left my phone in the car while I was there

I was ready to lose businessif it meant I could
have my life back

But that’s not what happened

What I expected:

  • my clients would leave because I wasn’t available at all hours of the day
  • I’d miss big opportunities by turning off notifications
  • I’d accomplish less by “wasting time” on non-business activities like hobbies and friendship

What happened:

  • my clients didn’t notice because I still got back to them within 24 hours
  • no opportunities were lost
  • I was getting significantly more work done

“How is it possible that you got more done by working fewer hours?”

First: the science

  • Working 60+ hours per week makes us less productive than working 40 hours1
  • Long hours destroy passion and engagement2
  • Overworking increases employee turnover3
  • Creativity suffers if we don’t go outside sometimes4
  • Lack of downtime hurts productivity and creativity5
  • We lose 20% of our time per task while multitasking6

5 Strategies to
Boost Productivity

  1. Passive time tracking
  2. Time-boxing tasks
  3. Front-loading demanding work
  4. Aggressive prioritization
  5. NO multitasking

#1: Passive Time Tracking

The simple act of acknowledging how we spend our time encourages us to use it more wisely.

If I have a long day...

...I know to take a day off

#2: Time-Box Tasks

You’re never more productive than when you’re on deadline with 13% battery left.

Find your rhythm.

  • Experiment with different lengths of time: 25 minutes (Pomodoro), 60, or 90 to start
  • Set a timer
  • Close and turn off everything that isn’t required for the task
  • Start the timer
  • Kick much ass
  • When the timer goes off, take a break

Why This Works

  • Without distractions, we get into a state of “flow”
  • The world will not implode if we’re not paying attention for 90 minutes
    • If things break down because you’re unavailable for 90 minutes, something is very wrong
  • Taking breaks is good for productivity and creativity (and health, if you’re into that)
  • Chat and email checks happen at regular intervals without taking over the day

Also: science

There’s something called an “ultradian rhythm”, which is roughly 90 minutes and regulates our energy.8

#3: Front-Load Demanding Work

Motivation and self-control are resources that deplete as the day goes on. Don’t waste them on bullshit.

We’re the best versions of ourselves early in our day

  • energy is higher
  • focus is improved
  • self-control is at its peak
This is NOT me saying you should wake up early. Wake up whenever you want; just do the important shit before you do anything else.

Evening Me

#4: Prioritize Aggressively

If everything is The Most Important Thing™, nothing is.

Use the Highlander Technique

Put tasks into a tournament bracket...

...and then start choosing.

#5: DO NOT Multitask

Ever. Just stop it.

Context Switching is fatal to productivity

Source: Coding Horror

Multitasking makes us
feel productive.

Single-tasking makes us actually productive.

Let’s Recap

If we work too much, we’re:

  • worse at our jobs
  • more likely to burn out
  • less likely to feel satisfied at our job
  • less able to enjoy our lives

If we work a reasonable schedule, we’re:

  • more creative
  • more productive
  • happier (both in and out of work)
  • healthier

We can be more productive in less time by:

  1. paying attention to how we spend our time
  2. time-boxing tasks to create urgency & limit distractions
  3. front-loading demanding tasks while our energy is high
  4. aggressively prioritizing work to enable better focus
  5. never, ever, ever multitasking again
