Seeking is the pursuit of a defined goal

William Recinos
  • Seeking is how we become great
  • Pick a goal and put the work in to achieve it
  • Anders Ericsson coined the term “deliberate practice” to describe it
  • Often oversimplified to “10,000 hours”

Seeking is limitedto what we know

  • It’s really difficult to set a goal that we don’t know exists
  • Think about pre-industrial business: people inherited the family business because that was what they knew

Seeking creates risk & fear

  • When you set a big goal, failure carries risk
  • Can lead to playing it safe
  • “If I might fail, it’s probably better not to try”
  • High confidence in a narrow discipline
  • Low confidence everywhere else
  • Fixed mindset

Fixed Mindset:

“I’m not the kind of person who can __________________.”

  • This is the wrong story to tell ourselves
  • We tend to live the stories we tell ourselves

Failure 

  • This is because we tend to have an “all is lost” mentality about failure
  • That’s simply not true
  • We need failure

FailureLearning

  • Failure is learning
  • Knowing what _doesn’t_ work can be just as helpful as knowing what does
  • Copter Labs story
  • Wanted to build an agency
  • Succeeded in a lot of ways: clients, made a living, some notoriety
  • Failed in so many ways: bad management, debt, stress, health
  • Ultimately ended up selling the company because I couldn’t handle it

Failure teaches resilience

  • Resilience is how we bounce back
  • Jason Comely was so convinced resilience was important that he set out to get rejected
Rejection Therapy
  • It worked so well he made a game out of it
  • I did my own version of rejection therapy
  • I sold vacuum cleaners door to door

  • I learned to toughen up

Takahiro Taguchi

  • “Killing time”
  • Not productive

Kelly Sikkema
  • But wandering is a critical part of our development
  • As kids, we play, follow our interests, and learn about “useless” stuff
  • My sister learned all the words to Balto
  • This shows us the doors we can walk through as adults
Stefan Steinbauer
  • If we never wander, if we never explore, we might not even realize the doors that are available to us
  • We _need_ to wander to know what our options are
  • Otherwise critical decisions about our life’s path will be made by default...
  • ...or by other people
Midjiwan
  • Wandering shows us possibilities
  • Like the map on some video games; we have to head to uknown areas to get access to more stuff
  • Each possibility is a new area to explore
  • If we keep exploring, we find _even more_ possibilities
  • This is what Steven Johnson calls “the adjacent possible”
Dil Assi
  • Doors analogy
  • Each door is a thing you’re capable of doing
  • All doors come with you
  • Doors don’t close; you’re just finding more doors
Debashis Biswas
  • One of the best examples of the adjacent possible is cooking
  • Cooking allows us to use significantly less energy to digest our food
  • The extra energy can be used by our brains
  • Learning to cook opened the door for humans to grow large brains
  • Example: when we learn to stand, it’s possible to learn to walk
  • Sometimes it creates a lot of possibilities: learning to talk means we can learn to tell stories, lies, jokes

Wandering builds confidence

  • Small successes in lots of different places add up
  • Bad: “I’ve been able to figure this thing out; maybe I’m only good at this thing.”
  • Good: “I’ve been able to figure out lots of stuff; I can probably figure this out, too.”

Wandering reduces risk

  • Many risks aren’t actually risks — unless you don’t have information
  • Wandering helps broaden our knowledge
  • Often that knowledge gives us confidence that a “risk” isn’t risky at all
Clint Adair
  • Wandering helps find connections
  • Our subconscious is _really_ good at finding new connections between ideas
  • But it only works if we shut off the conscious part of our brains
  • Requires disconnection from devices, stimulation, etc.
  • This is why we often solve problems in the shower or in dreams

We need both seeking & wandering

Always seek intelligently

  • If we seek too long, it feels hopeless
  • No rewards; everything is “in the future”
  • Target feels impossible
  • Set realistic goals, and break them up if necessary

Actionable
Timely
Ownable
Measurable

  • Actionable — it can be worked on now
  • Time-Restricted — it has a short duration
  • Ownable — it has a single owner (you!)
  • Measurable — it has a clear definition of done

Wander oftenbut take action

  • Set aside time to explore new ideas
  • Make sure to actually follow through and do the work
  • This will create small successes that build momentum
